Get better results

Tips and tricks for stronger agent output.

These habits separate agents that produce impressive output every time from agents that occasionally disappoint.

Always confirm aim and audience first

Before asking your agent to write any content, confirm the presentation's aim and who is in the room. A two-sentence brief — "The aim is to get board approval for a $50K budget increase. The audience is six board members who are supportive but budget-conscious" — produces dramatically better output than launching straight into a content request.

Give it the most important number first

If your presentation has a key figure — a budget, a result, a deadline, a risk — state it in your brief. The agent will lead with it. "Revenue is $487K, up 8% on the same quarter last year" is more useful than "revenue is strong this quarter."

Reject and refine in conversation

If a slide title is too vague, say: "The title on Slide 3 does not state the message clearly — rewrite it as a complete sentence that names the finding." The agent learns from your feedback within the conversation. Each refinement improves the output for the rest of the session.

Use the P-PASS check explicitly

After the agent delivers a draft, ask: "Run the P-PASS quality check on this presentation and flag anything that does not pass." The agent will systematically check Purpose, Persuasion, Audience, Safety, and Standards — and surface issues you might miss on a first read.

Update your instruction after every session

If you notice the agent consistently missing something — a required section, a tone element, a constraint — add it to your system instruction and re-paste it. Your instruction is a living document. It should improve every time you use the agent.

Upload updated documents regularly

Your knowledge base is only as current as the files you have uploaded. If you have a new report template, updated policy, or revised pricing document — re-upload it. Remove outdated versions to avoid the agent referencing stale information.

Prompt ideas by sector

Starting prompts for your professional context.

Adapt these to your specific context. The more specific your brief, the more specific the output.

Accounting & Finance
  • "Create a quarterly financial report for Q3 FY2025 — revenue $487K, expenses $299K, net profit $188K, GST due 28 April $44,270. Audience: two small business owners, non-financial."
  • "Draft a year-end tax planning presentation for a client with a $320K taxable income — highlight three strategies to reduce liability before 30 June."
  • "Build a cash flow forecast presentation for a business with $143K in bank and $67K in outstanding debtors. Three action items required."
Education
  • "Create a professional development presentation on AI in the classroom for a staff meeting. Audience: 25 primary school teachers, mixed digital confidence. Aim: leave with one practical action to try this week."
  • "Draft a parent information evening presentation about Year 6 assessment changes. Plain English, no jargon, warm tone. Maximum 8 slides."
  • "Build a board update presentation on student achievement data for Term 2. Audience: school board members. Aim: approval for additional literacy support funding."
NFP & Community
  • "Create a funding proposal presentation for a $75,000 grant application. Audience: government funding panel. Aim: demonstrate community impact and organisational capability."
  • "Draft a community engagement presentation about a new youth program launching in Logan. Audience: local parents and community members. Warm, accessible tone."
  • "Build a stakeholder impact report presentation for our annual general meeting. Audience: board, volunteers, and community partners. Highlight three outcomes from this year."
Healthcare & Allied Health
  • "Create a practice update presentation for staff on new Medicare billing changes effective 1 July. Audience: reception and clinical staff. Practical and clear — no regulatory jargon."
  • "Draft a patient information presentation on managing Type 2 diabetes through lifestyle changes. Plain English, supportive tone, maximum 6 slides."
  • "Build a referral pathway presentation for GPs about our allied health services. Professional, evidence-based, concise. Aim: increase referrals by clearly communicating scope of practice."
Government & Local Council
  • "Create a community consultation presentation on the proposed park upgrade in Marsden. Audience: local residents, mixed age group. Aim: gather feedback and explain the proposal clearly."
  • "Draft a budget briefing presentation for councillors on the infrastructure maintenance program for FY2026. Formal tone, data-led, decision required at the meeting."
  • "Build a program evaluation presentation on the youth employment initiative — 12-month results, outcomes against targets, recommendations for Year 2."
Small Business & SME
  • "Create a pitch presentation for a commercial lease negotiation. Business: Logan-based retail store, 5-year lease renewal. Audience: commercial property manager. Aim: secure a reduced rate."
  • "Draft a team briefing presentation on the new rostering system launching next month. Audience: 12 staff members. Practical, step-by-step, no jargon."
  • "Build a supplier proposal presentation. We want to onboard a new materials supplier. Audience: our operations director. Aim: approval to switch suppliers by end of month."
Before you present

Quality check — six questions before you finalise.

Run through these before using any AI-generated presentation output. They take two minutes and prevent the most common errors.

1 — Does every slide title state the message?

A slide title should be a complete sentence that names the finding, recommendation, or argument — not a topic label. "Market Analysis" fails. "The Australian market grew 12% in FY2025 but our share declined for the third consecutive quarter" passes.

2 — Is the most important point first?

Check that the deck leads with the key message — not background, not methodology, not context. If someone reads only the first two slides and the title of every subsequent slide, they should understand the full argument.

3 — Is the language right for this audience?

Read each slide as if you are the least experienced person in the room. Is every technical term explained? Are all acronyms defined on first use? Would a parent, a board member, or a small business owner understand this without a glossary?

4 — Is every claim supported by evidence?

Check that every data point, statistic, and claim is either sourced from your uploaded documents or clearly identified as an estimate. Unsupported claims in a presentation create reputational risk — particularly in financial, legal, and government contexts.

5 — Is there a clear ask on the final slide?

Every presentation should close with a specific, actionable commitment. "In conclusion, AI offers significant opportunities" is not an ask. "Approve the $50K budget for the AI pilot program by Friday 20 June" is.

6 — Would you stake your professional reputation on this?

Read the full deck one more time as if your most critical professional peer is in the room. If any slide gives you pause — rewrite it. The agent produced a draft. You are the professional. The final judgement is yours.

Beyond yourself

Scaling your agent across your team and organisation.

The real value of a configured agent is not what it does for one person — it is what happens when it serves an entire team consistently.

Deploy to your team via Microsoft Teams

Once your agent is configured and tested, publish it to your Microsoft 365 tenant. It will appear in Microsoft Teams for everyone in your organisation with access.

  • Navigate to the agent publishing settings in M365
  • Select the colleagues or groups who should have access
  • The agent appears in their Teams sidebar under Copilot or Apps
  • No individual configuration required — everyone uses the same agent

What consistent deployment achieves

  • Consistent quality — every team member produces presentations that meet the same professional standard, regardless of their individual AI skill level
  • Organisational voice — your key messages, tone, and non-negotiables are embedded — not dependent on any individual
  • New staff onboarding — new team members are productive from day one. The agent carries the institutional knowledge.
  • One update, everyone benefits — improve the system instruction once and the whole team gets it immediately

Governance — who owns the instruction?

Nominate one person to own and maintain the system instruction — typically a senior team member or operations lead. They review the instruction quarterly, update it when professional standards change, and manage the knowledge base documents. Without ownership, instructions become outdated silently.

Version control — track your changes

Keep a dated copy of your system instruction every time you make a significant change. Save them as: Agent Instruction v1 — June 2026, v2 — August 2026 and so on. If an update produces worse output, you can roll back immediately.

Measure the impact

After deploying to your team, track: time spent per presentation before and after, number of revision rounds required, and team confidence in AI tools. These three metrics tell you whether the agent is delivering genuine organisational value — and give you the evidence to expand it further.

The difference between a tool and an organisational capability is whether one person uses it or whether the whole organisation benefits from it consistently. You have built the tool. Deploy it and it becomes the capability.
